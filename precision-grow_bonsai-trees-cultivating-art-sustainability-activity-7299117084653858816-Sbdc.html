This review shows that circularity isn’t a silver bullet, but when incorporated into broader climate strategies involving consequential stakeholder engagement, it can be a powerful tool. Otherwise, circular economy strategies risk resistance. On average, circular economy strategies (e.g. closing loops and slowing resource flows) have around 17% greenhouse gas (GHG) mitigation potential on their own. However, when combined with energy efficiency and decarbonisation, this figure could increase to around 50% (within a range of 1–100%). Transitions to the circular economy generate winners and losers — for example, regions or sectors dependent on resource extraction could face disruption. The political dimension therefore involves distributing the costs and benefits amongst stakeholders (e.g. firms, workers, and communities). Link: https://lnkd.in/efpEvgu4
